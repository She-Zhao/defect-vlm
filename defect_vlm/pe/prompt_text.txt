# Defect Definitions & Imaging Characteristics
The following are the visual characteristics of common defects under this light source:
* **Scratch**: Appears as a narrow, subtle dark linear structure, with shapes being straight, curved, or irregular waves. A scratch stands out as a high-frequency dark shadow independent of the background light and shadow distribution, like a mark "carved" on a smoothly graduating striped background. Its sharp lines contrast distinctly with the soft light and dark stripes of the background.
* **Breakage**: Appears as a point-like or irregular patch-like physical damage within a very small local range. Distinguishable from the smooth transition of a crater, breakage has rough, jagged, or radial sharp edges. Under striped light, the defect core loses mirror reflection capability due to the lack of paint material, appearing as a high-contrast black "blind spot" or hole. The main feature is the abrupt interruption, absence, or discontinuity of the stripe texture here.
* **Crater**: Appears as a concave geometric distortion in a local area. The stripes at the defect center present a vortex-like, spiral, or inward-contracting converging form, like water being sucked into a whirlpool, causing the stripes to twist and knot violently. The center is usually accompanied by a high-contrast dark shadow spot caused by deep depression.
* **Bulge**: Appears as a convex geometric distortion in a local area. The originally straight or smoothly transitioning light and dark stripes expand, bend, or diverge outward at the defect, like contour lines on a map wrapping around a peak. The stripe direction is significantly "stretched open," destroying the linear consistency of the background stripes.
* **Run**: Appears as a drooping, teardrop-shaped, or wavy paint accumulation area caused by gravity. Under striped light, it presents as a large-area, smoothly transitioning convex distortion. The light and dark stripes at the defect undergo wide-range smooth bending and enveloping conforming to the flow direction of the droplet (like contour lines wrapping around a round hill). Compared to a normal bulge, its stripe distortion has obvious vertical extension and a sense of volumetric expansion.
* **Inclusion**: Appears as a tiny granular or nodular foreign object inclusion embedded in the surface or interior of the paint layer. Unlike large-area geometric deformations, inclusions usually appear as isolated point-like optical distortions under striped light. Due to their tiny convex structure, they often form halo effects similar to concentric circles, Newton's rings, or accompanying central bright spots/dark cores, causing sharp ripple-like disturbances in the background stripes within a minimal range, like tiny gravel falling onto a calm water surface.
