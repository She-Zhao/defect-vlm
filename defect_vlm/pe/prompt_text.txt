# Defect Definitions & Visual Characteristics
The following are the visual characteristics of common defects under this light source:
* **Scratch**: Appears as a narrow, fine, dark linear structure, with shapes that are straight, curved, or irregularly wavy. As a high-frequency dark shadow independent of the background light and shadow distribution, it looks like a mark "carved" onto the smooth gradient stripe background. Its sharp linearity contrasts sharply with the soft light and dark stripes of the background.
* **Breakage**: Appears as point-like or irregular patch-like physical damage within an extremely small local area. Distinguished from the smooth transition of a crater, breakage has rough, jagged, or radial sharp edges (like tiny chipping on a glass surface). Under stripe light, the defect core loses specular reflection capability due to missing paint material, appearing as a high-contrast black "blind spot" or void. The main feature is the sudden interruption, absence, or discontinuity of the stripe texture here, rather than smooth geometric bending.
* **Crater**: Appears as a local concave geometric distortion. Stripes at the defect center present a vortex-like, spiral, or inward-shrinking converging morphology, like water being sucked into a whirlpool, causing the stripes to undergo severe twisting and knotting. The center is usually accompanied by a high-contrast shadow dark spot formed by deep depression.
* **Bulge**: Appears as a local convex geometric distortion. The originally straight or smoothly transitioning light and dark stripes undergo outward expansion, bending, or divergence at the defect, resembling contour lines on a map wrapping around a mountain peak. The stripe direction is significantly "stretched open," destroying the linear consistency of the background stripes.
* **Run**: Appears as a sagging, teardrop-shaped, or wave-like accumulation of paint liquid formed under the influence of gravity. Under stripe light, it presents as a large-area, smoothly transitioning convex distortion. The light and dark stripes at the defect undergo wide-amplitude smooth bending and enveloping conforming to the flow direction of the droplet (like contour lines wrapping a rounded hill). Compared to a normal bulge, its stripe distortion has obvious vertical extension and a sense of volumetric expansion.
* **Inclusion**: Appears as a tiny granular or nodular foreign body inclusion embedded in the surface layer or interior of the paint. Unlike large-area geometric deformations, inclusions usually appear as isolated point-like optical distortions under stripe light. Due to their tiny convex structure, they often form halo effects similar to concentric circles, Newton's rings, or accompanied by a central bright spot/dark core, causing acute ripple-like disturbances in the background stripes within an extremely small range, like a tiny grain of sand falling into calm water.
